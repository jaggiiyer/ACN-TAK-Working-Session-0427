Program Governance & Leadership Activation
EXPAND ↓
Where Takeda stands today
You've got a SteerCo today — is it working? or as this scales, does that model still hold? Should this run through one forum like RMC, or do you need something more distributed? And how well is the structure below it really holding up?
Right now, it doesn't feel like there's a single, integrated plan — or a clear sense of sequencing and how things move from one stage to the next.
Leadership support is present in the room but not yet translated into active accountability outside of it
Our perspective on best practice
A transformation of this scale needs three layers — enterprise governance setting direction, a PMO coordinating cross-use case delivery, and project-level execution with clear standards
Governance without leadership activation is just process — the right people need to be genuinely accountable, visibly sponsoring, and willing to make trade-offs when priorities conflict

Value Management
EXPAND ↓
Where Takeda stands today
The ~$80M target is set at the portfolio level, with some breakdown across functions.
Limited baseline metrics, no leading indicators, no kill/scale criteria at the use case level
There is some value expected in FY27, but more than half sits in FY28. Looking at this more end-to-end might open up more options — especially if some things don't work out early.
Our perspective on best practice
Every use case needs a discrete value hypothesis, a baseline, and leading and lagging indicators defined before delivery begins — not after
Kill/scale criteria need to be explicit and agreed upfront — what does success look like at 90 days, and what would tell you to stop before you've over-invested
Value tracking should be a program discipline, not a reporting exercise — it informs sequencing decisions in real time
Questions
1

How would you know today whether you're on track to the 80 OY target — is there an early signal, or is FY28 the first real checkpoint?

2

What's the current logic for scaling something that's working — or stopping something that isn't?

3

Who owns value tracking at the program level — and does that person have the authority to act on what they're seeing?

Operating Model Blueprint
EXPAND ↓
Where Takeda stands today
Use cases are being designed without a shared picture of the future state they're building toward
No integrated view of how R&D mega-processes work end to end, how work gets orchestrated, or how decisions flow
Without it, use cases risk being point solutions rather than building blocks of something coherent
Our perspective on best practice
Before you can sequence use cases meaningfully, you need a high-level blueprint of the future state — the core R&D mega-processes, how work flows across them, where decisions are made and by whom
This blueprint shows where AI and automation embed into the process — not alongside it
It doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be shared. It's the orienting picture everything else navigates by
Questions
1

Is there a single integrated picture of what R&D looks like in the future state — one that the whole program is building toward?

2

How are use cases being connected to the end-to-end process today — or are they being designed in isolation?

3

Who owns the responsibility for making sure the pieces add up to a coherent operating model?

Innovation & Experimentation
EXPAND ↓
Where Takeda stands today
Use cases do go through POC and pilot phases — but these aren't yet tied to a formal kill/scale decision framework at the program level
Experimentation happens within use cases, but there's no structured space to test ideas before they enter the delivery pipeline
Without formal reporting of pilot outcomes, it's hard to know when to stop before over-investing
Our perspective on best practice
The POC/pilot instinct is right — the opportunity is to make it more formal: defined success criteria upfront, structured reporting, and explicit go/no-go decisions before scaling
Pilot outcomes should feed directly into wave sequencing and the operating model blueprint — not sit in isolation
A lightweight innovation space — distinct from the delivery pipeline — lets new ideas get tested without consuming program capacity
Questions
1

How are pilot outcomes being tracked and reported — is there a consistent mechanism, or does it vary by use case?

2

What's the explicit trigger to stop a pilot before it becomes a full delivery commitment?

3

Where do new ideas that aren't yet in the pipeline get tested — is there a home for early-stage experimentation?

Workforce Transformation & Change
EXPAND ↓
Where Takeda stands today
Change management is not yet a program discipline — no integrated change plan, no readiness assessment, no adoption tracking across use cases
Workforce transformation needs to be designed in from the start — not added at the end
Role impacts from AI-enabled use cases are not yet being planned for at the program level
Our perspective on best practice
Change management needs to be a first-class program workstream — with its own plan, resourcing, and accountability — not something added when adoption stalls
Role redesign and capability building should run in parallel with use case delivery, not sequentially after it
Adoption tracking should be as rigorous as value tracking — if people aren't using what's been built, the value case doesn't close
Questions
1

Who is responsible for making sure people actually adopt what gets built — and is that person resourced to do it?

2

How are role impacts and capability needs being identified and planned for as use cases go live?

3

Is there a change plan that runs alongside the delivery plan — or is change being treated as something that happens after?

Solution Architecture & Delivery
EXPAND ↓
Where Takeda stands today
Use cases are being built without a clear view of shared technical foundations — data standards, integration architecture, and AI model governance are not yet explicit at the program level
Risk of duplicating effort and accumulating technical debt across waves
DD&T tech and AI workstream needs to move now — some of these implementations won't take long once foundations are clear
Our perspective on best practice
Shared technical foundations — data standards, integration patterns, AI model governance — need to be defined early, not retrofitted later
Reusable components and shared data pipelines reduce delivery time across waves and prevent each use case from rebuilding from scratch
Architecture decisions made now will constrain or enable everything that comes after — deferring them is not a neutral choice
Questions
1

Is there a shared technical foundation that use cases are building on — or is each one starting from scratch?

2

Where are the reusable components, shared data pipelines, and common standards that reduce duplication across waves?

3

How is DD&T's tech and AI workstream sequenced relative to use case delivery — are they moving in parallel, or is architecture following rather than leading?

How DD&T needs to
evolve to sustain and scale

AIDPA will only scale if DD&T is willing to change — not just what it builds, but how it operates, where it leads, and who it holds itself accountable to.

Contributions
  • Actively shape the ambition — not just receive it — by injecting what is technically feasible, in what timeframe, and at what cost
  • Ground outcomes in data and platform reality before commitments are made
  • Establish DD&T's voice as a co-author of ambition, not a downstream enabler
  • Turn the ambition into a clear, sequenced plan (what happens first, what follows, and why)
  • Articulate what this depends on (across data, tech, and the functions)
  • Design room to test and learn and scale what works
  • Build transparency on timelines, cost, and risks (so trade-offs can be made early)
  • Make progress visible, including how DD&T and the functions are actually working together
  • Bring the right teams together to deliver end-to-end (Product owners/BSOs, Product Mgmt, Data Modeling, Data Engineering, Security, Architecture)
  • Focus on a few priority workflows and move fast from concept to impact
  • Test, learn, and adjust quickly, rather than over-design upfront
  • Capture what works and reuse it, so each effort builds on the last
  • Set clear guardrails on architecture and data, speed shouldn't create rework later
  • Design adoption around a hybrid WoW
  • Tailor training with more self enablement/paced options
  • Evolve DD&T's own delivery roles to keep up with the pace of change
Skills & Talent
  • Domain fluency — R&D process, regulatory context, and what AI can realistically change
  • Ability to translate emerging technology into grounded outcomes (cycle time, quality, cost)
  • Early product thinking — scoping and sequencing before requirements are fully formed
  • Strong program leadership — able to drive and sequence complex, cross-functional work
  • Architecture and data judgment — enough to make the right calls early and avoid rework
  • Comfort with cost, value, and trade-offs as a co-equal partner — not just support
  • Starts building: product management capability to define use cases, prioritize, and own delivery
  • Strong engineering depth — data pipelines, AI/ML, LLM engineering, prompt design
  • Hands-on delivery mindset — building and iterating, not coordinating
  • Ability to work across functions and capture reusable patterns
  • Comfort with ambiguity — test, learn, adjust without over-designing upfront
  • Starts building: LLMOps, data curation, compliance-by-design — learned here, load-bearing in Phase 4
  • Product ownership at scale — DD&T defines use cases, prioritizes the roadmap, drives iteration
  • LLMOps — monitoring models, managing prompts, governing AI outputs in production
  • Risk and compliance built into how solutions are delivered, not added after
  • Self-service tooling and enablement — DD&T builds for scale, not bespoke delivery
  • Ability to shape the next wave of ambition — the cycle begins again
